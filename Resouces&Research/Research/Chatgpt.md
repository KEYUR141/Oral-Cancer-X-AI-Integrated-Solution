# Oral Cancer Progression: From Normal Cell to Advanced Cancer

## Important Terms

### Squamous Epithelial Cells
The mouth is covered by a protective lining called the oral epithelium.

The cells that make up this lining are called squamous epithelial cells.

Functions:
- Protect underlying tissues
- Resist friction from chewing and speaking
- Prevent harmful substances from easily reaching deeper tissues
- Continuously replace damaged cells

Most oral cancers begin in these cells.

---

### DNA Mutation
A mutation is a change in the DNA of a cell.

Causes include:
- Smoking
- Tobacco chewing
- Gutkha
- Betel nut (supari)
- Alcohol
- HPV infection

Repeated exposure damages DNA inside squamous epithelial cells.

---

## Stage 1: DNA Damage and Early Cellular Changes

Normal squamous epithelial cells are repeatedly exposed to carcinogens.

Over time:
- DNA becomes damaged
- Mutations accumulate
- Cells begin losing normal growth control

At this stage:
- No visible symptoms
- No cancer yet
- Changes occur at the microscopic level

---

## Stage 2: Dysplasia (Precancerous Stage)

### Dysplasia
Dysplasia means abnormal cell growth.

Characteristics:
- Cells look abnormal
- Cells divide faster than normal
- DNA damage increases
- Tissue organization becomes irregular

Dysplasia is NOT cancer.

However, it can eventually become cancer if untreated.

### Common Precancerous Lesions

#### Leukoplakia
A white patch inside the mouth.

Characteristics:
- Cannot be scraped off
- Often linked to tobacco use
- May contain dysplasia

#### Erythroplakia
A red patch inside the mouth.

Characteristics:
- Less common than leukoplakia
- Higher risk of becoming cancer
- Often contains severe dysplasia

At this stage:
- Treatment can often prevent cancer development.

---

## Stage 3: Carcinoma In Situ (Stage 0 Cancer)

### Meaning

Carcinoma:
Cancer arising from epithelial cells.

In Situ:
"In its original place."

At this stage:
- The entire epithelial layer contains cancerous cells.
- Cancer cells have NOT invaded deeper tissues.
- The basement membrane remains intact.

Think of it as cancer that is still confined to the surface layer.

Treatment success is usually very high.

---

## Stage 4: Invasive Oral Cancer

Cancer cells break through the basement membrane.

Now they can invade:
- Tongue
- Floor of mouth
- Gums
- Inner cheeks
- Palate

Characteristics:
- Tissue destruction begins
- Tumor formation occurs
- Cancer can access lymphatic vessels

### Common Signs

#### Ulcer
An open sore that does not heal.

Unlike normal mouth ulcers:
- Persists for weeks
- May bleed
- Often has hard or raised edges

#### Lump
A mass formed by rapidly multiplying cancer cells.

Characteristics:
- Hard texture
- Continues growing
- May interfere with chewing or speaking

---

## Stage 5: Spread to Lymph Nodes

### Lymph Node
A small immune-system structure that filters lymphatic fluid.

Cancer cells can enter lymphatic vessels and travel to nearby lymph nodes.

Most commonly affected:
- Neck lymph nodes
- Cervical lymph nodes
- Submandibular lymph nodes

Signs:
- Enlarged lymph nodes
- Hard neck swelling

This usually represents a more advanced stage of disease.

---

## Stage 6: Metastasis (Distant Spread)

### Metastasis
The spread of cancer to distant organs.

Cancer cells may enter:
- Blood vessels
- Lymphatic vessels

Possible destinations:
- Lungs (most common)
- Liver
- Bones

This is the most advanced stage of cancer.

---

# Clinical Staging

## Stage 0
Carcinoma In Situ

- Cancer only in the epithelial layer
- No invasion

## Stage I
Early invasive cancer

- Small tumor
- No lymph node involvement

## Stage II
Larger tumor

- Still localized
- No major spread

## Stage III
Regional spread

- Larger tumor
- Nearby lymph node involvement

## Stage IV
Advanced cancer

- Extensive local invasion
OR
- Multiple lymph nodes involved
OR
- Distant metastasis
